🗒️ Guide
Here is an additional guide and context for each item, so you better understand what I want -- be sure to take it into account:
Main Problem:
This is the central, dominant problem in the lives of my potential clients. It is the urgent, pressing problem that my potential client is currently facing. Which they want to solve. Which they cannot postpone. It is also what our product or service is designed to help with. Examples: elderly people suffering from constant knee pain that prevents them from sitting or walking for long, single men struggling with dating attractive women who feel inadequate because of it, coaches who can't attract clients, office workers stuck in the "hamster wheel," living on a work-home-work program. This problem needs to be described in detail and emotionally; this is very important and affects the success of the entire analysis.
Pain Situations:
A client's pain situation is the moment when a person realizes they have a problem that prevents them from living the way they want, and it causes them discomfort, pain, or fear. This is the point where they understand they can no longer ignore the problem and are ready to act to solve it. It is at this moment that the client starts looking for a solution, and if your product or service offers them a way out of this situation, the likelihood of purchase significantly increases.
Important: The description of the pain situation must be maximally detailed and close to the real experience of your target audience. It should reflect their specific problems, fears, and emotions. It must be described very specifically so the client recognizes themselves in it. The pain situation must be relevant to the client here and now. It must describe what they are experiencing at this moment.
Example of a detailed pain situation: I've been trying to get pregnant for several years, but every month I see one line on the test. I've gone through every possible examination, and doctors say everything is fine. But pregnancy still doesn't happen. I feel hope slipping away, and pressure from relatives only intensifies. Every time I see a pregnant woman on the street, it hurts. I ask myself: 'Why does everyone else succeed but not me?' I'm already tired of endless tests and examinations. I feel that your body is letting you down, and you don't understand what to do next.
Key Obstacles and Myths on the Path to Solving the Problem:
A client's key obstacle is a specific problem, barrier, or difficulty that prevents a person from advancing from their current state (point A, where they recognize their pain or problem) to the desired result (point B, where the problem is solved). It is not the pain itself, but what stands in the way of solving it. The key obstacle is often related to a lack of knowledge, skills, resources, or confidence needed to achieve the goal. It can be both external (e.g., lack of money, time, or information) and internal (fears, doubts, false beliefs).
Important: key obstacles arise when a person has ALREADY STARTED doing something toward solving the problem but isn't succeeding, or they want to start but don't know how. As a rule, the most solvent audience -- those already taking action -- is found at this stage.
The description of a key obstacle should be:
Specific: Clearly indicate what exactly is preventing the client.
Emotional: Show what feelings this obstacle causes.
Relevant: Correspond to the client's real experience.
Clear: Be simple and straightforward so the client can say: "That's about me."
Example:
"You want to become an online fitness trainer, but you don't know how to sell your first training session. You're afraid that nobody will want to train with you, and you don't understand how to promote yourself on social media. This causes you a feeling of insecurity and fear that you're wasting your time."
Client Myths:
Myths are false beliefs or fears that prevent the client from acting. They often arise from lack of information, negative experience, or stereotypes. Or they are established belief stereotypes in the field that the client believes due to lack of awareness or experience. For example, every fitness trainer knows that if you run a lot, it doesn't necessarily mean you'll lose weight. However, every person who is just starting to work out thinks the more they run and the more they sweat, the more weight they lose. That is, myths are what the client believes and what they usually start doing out of ignorance; then they might fail, get disappointed, and look for a solution elsewhere. However, the myth is always in the client's head, and they think that's how it should be. For example, in the marketing niche, a myth is that you can set certain specific ad settings and get paying clients. Myths should also be described in maximum detail because this is exactly what we use to hook our clients and what we promise, because it creates in them the desire to buy.
