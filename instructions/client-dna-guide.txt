Here is an additional guide and context for each point, so you better understand what I want — make sure to account for it:
Main problem:
This is the central, dominant problem in the lives of my potential clients. This is an urgent pressing problem that my potential client is currently facing. One they want to solve. One they cannot postpone. This is also what our product or service is designed to help with. Examples: elderly people suffering from constant knee pain that prevents them from sitting and walking for long, single men struggling with dating attractive women who feel inadequate because of it, coaches who can't attract clients, office workers stuck in the "hamster wheel," living on the work-home-work program. This problem needs to be described in detail and emotionally; this is very important and affects the success of the entire analysis.
Pain situations:
A client's pain situation is the moment when a person realizes they have a problem that prevents them from living the way they want, and this causes them discomfort, pain, or fear. This is the point where they understand they can no longer ignore the problem and are ready to act to solve it. It is at this moment that the client starts looking for a solution, and if your product or service offers them a way out of this situation, the likelihood of purchase increases significantly.
Important: The description of the pain situation should be as detailed as possible and close to the real experience of your target audience. It should reflect their specific problems, fears, and emotions. It should be described very specifically so the client recognizes themselves in it. The pain situation must be relevant to the client here and now. It should describe what they are experiencing at this moment.
Example of a detailed pain situation: I've been trying to get pregnant for several years now, but every month I see one line on the test. I've gone through every possible examination, and doctors say everything is fine. But pregnancy still doesn't happen. I feel hope slipping away, and pressure from relatives only intensifies. Every time I see a pregnant woman on the street, it hurts. I ask myself: 'Why does everyone else succeed, but not me?' I'm already tired of endless tests and examinations. I feel like your body is failing you, and you don't understand what to do next.
Key obstacles and myths on the path to solving the problem:
A client's key obstacle is a specific problem, barrier, or difficulty that prevents a person from moving from their current state (point A, where they recognize their pain or problem) to the desired result (point B, where the problem is solved). This is not the pain itself, but what stands in the way of solving it. A key obstacle is often related to a lack of knowledge, skills, resources, or confidence needed to achieve the goal. It can be both external (for example, lack of money, time, or information) and internal (fears, doubts, false beliefs).
Important: key obstacles arise when a person has ALREADY STARTED doing something toward solving the problem, but it's not working, or they want to start but don't know how. As a rule, this is where the most solvent audience is found — people who are already taking action.
The description of a key obstacle should be:
Specific: Clearly identify what exactly is preventing the client.
Emotional: Show what feelings this obstacle causes.
Relevant: Match the client's real experience.
Clear: Be simple and understandable so the client can say: "That's me."
Example:
"You want to become an online fitness trainer, but you don't know how to sell your first training session. You're afraid no one will want to train with you, and you don't understand how to promote yourself on social media. This causes you feelings of insecurity and fear that you're wasting your time."
Client myths:
Myths are false beliefs or fears that prevent the client from taking action. They often arise from a lack of information, negative experience, or stereotypes. Or, these are established belief stereotypes in the field that the client believes due to lack of awareness or experience. For example, every fitness trainer knows that if you run a lot, it doesn't necessarily mean you'll lose weight. However, every person who just starts training thinks that the more they run, the more they sweat, the more weight they lose. That is, myths are what the client believes, and what they usually start doing out of ignorance, then they possibly fail at something, get disappointed, and look for a solution to the problem elsewhere. However, the myth is always in the client's head and they think that's how it should be. For example, in the marketing niche, a myth is that you can set certain specific advertising settings and get solvent clients. Myths should also be described in maximum detail, because this is exactly what we use to hook our clients, and this is what we promise, because it creates their desire to buy.

A slow metabolism can give them an excuse not to try to stay fit. So, what satisfaction must the client let go of to solve their problem?

What external forces do clients blame for their problem? This could be the government, food corporations, their bosses, their parents, what gurus taught them. We're looking for both internal and external factors. My clients' perceived limitations, as well as external forces that negatively contribute to the problem.
